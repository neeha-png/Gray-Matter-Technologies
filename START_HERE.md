# Start Here — What You're Actually Building (Plain English)

This is the "read this first" guide. No jargon, no tech-speak — just what each
of the 10 projects really is, in everyday terms, and why it comes where it does.
Once something below makes sense to you, go open that project's folder — the
`README.md` inside it has the technical how-to.

They're numbered in the order you should do them, because each one uses
something you just learned in the one before it.

---

### 1. Look at real data like a detective (Module 1)
You'll grab a real dataset — for example, the list of passengers on the
Titanic — and just explore it. Who was on board? Who survived? Does age or
class seem to matter? You're not building anything fancy yet, you're just
asking questions of the data and using simple charts to find the answers.
Think of it as your first case as a data detective.

### 2. Clean up messy data (Module 2)
Real data is almost never neat. Some entries are missing, some are
duplicated, some are recorded in weird formats, and sometimes the
information you need is split across two different files. This project is
about tidying all of that up — like cleaning and organizing a messy
spreadsheet — so the data is actually usable for anything you do with it later.

### 3. Turn findings into a simple presentation (Module 2)
Using the cleaned-up data from project 2, you'll dig a bit deeper and find a
handful of genuinely interesting patterns — then present them simply, with a
chart and a one-line takeaway for each. It's the difference between "I
looked at some data" and "here's what I found, and here's proof."

### 4. Teach the computer to make predictions (Module 3)
This is where things get exciting: you'll teach the computer to predict
something from data — like whether a passenger survived, or what a house
might sell for. You'll try a few different prediction "recipes," see which
one guesses best, and then fine-tune it to make it even more accurate.

### 5. Put your prediction "recipe" into a simple app (Module 3)
Now you take that prediction recipe from project 4 and give it a face:
a tiny app where someone types in a few details and instantly gets a
prediction back. Like a calculator, except instead of doing math, it makes
an educated guess.

### 6. Build a smarter kind of AI (Module 4)
This project steps up to more advanced AI — the kind that can recognize
what's in a photo, or make sense of a sequence of text or numbers over time.
This is closer to what people usually picture when they hear "AI."

### 7. Turn that smarter AI into something you can use (Module 4)
Same idea as project 5, but for the AI from project 6: you wrap it in a
simple app. Upload a photo, or type in some text, and the app tells you what
it "thinks" it's looking at.

### 8. Solve a real business problem (Module 5)
Pick one realistic situation a company might actually care about — is this
customer review positive or negative? what product should we suggest to this
shopper? — and build something that answers it. You'll also make your
answer available in a way other programs could plug into and use.

### 9. Wrap it into a real, usable product (Module 5)
Take the business solution from project 8 and turn it into an actual live
app — something a real person could open, use, and get a result from
immediately, without knowing anything about how it works underneath. This is
the closest thing to a genuine mini product you'll have built so far.

### 10. Make it "real world ready" — the big final project (Module 6)
This is the capstone: take one of your finished apps (ideally project 9) and
get it ready for the real world. That means putting it on the internet so
anyone can reach it, packaging it up neatly so it runs anywhere, and adding a
smart chatbot-style feature on top. This project ties everything from the
whole program together, and doubles as your prep for the final assessment.

---

## Quick recap
- Projects 1–3: understanding and cleaning data
- Projects 4–5: making and using predictions
- Projects 6–7: more advanced "smart" AI (images/text)
- Projects 8–9: solving a real problem and turning it into a usable app
- Project 10: making everything production-ready, all in one final project

Once a project above makes sense to you, head into its numbered folder and
open the `README.md` there for the step-by-step technical details.
